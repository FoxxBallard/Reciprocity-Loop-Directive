**Next Expected Update:** *How's Life?* is biennial — next edition expected 2026
**Relevance to Directive:** Measures the 11 dimensions of quality of life

in non-material aspects of life that aggregate economic metrics miss entirely.

---

## What This Index Measures

The BLI covers 38 OECD member countries across 11 dimensions: Housing, 
Income, Jobs, Community, Education, Environment, Civic Engagement, Health, 
Life Satisfaction, Safety, and Work-Life Balance. Unlike most indexes it is 
interactive — users weight the dimensions according to their own priorities, 
generating a personalized ranking. The companion report *How's Life?* 
provides the deeper analytical layer, using 80+ indicators to assess trends, 
inequalities, and sustainability of wellbeing over time. The 2024 edition 
is the sixth, covering trends from 2010 to 2023.

## Global Summary

The *How's Life? 2024* report finds that while government interventions 
cushioned the economic impacts of COVID-19 and the cost-of-living crisis — 
keeping average incomes stable and employment at historic highs — significant 
warning signs are emerging in non-economic aspects of wellbeing, particularly 
health, subjective wellbeing, and social connectedness.  The picture is one 
of a two-track recovery: material conditions held relatively steady, but the 
inner and relational dimensions of human life are quietly deteriorating across 
much of the OECD. Nearly one in five low-income households are 
overburdened by housing costs, and almost one in five people reported financial 
difficulties in 2023.  Environmental sustainability remains a serious 
unresolved challenge across virtually all member countries.

## Areas of Concern Most Relevant to the Directive

**Subjective wellbeing and social connectedness** are the dimensions most 
directly relevant to the Reciprocity Loop. A society where people are 
increasingly lonely, worried, and sad — even if employed and housed — is 
not a society producing the conditions for genuine human-AI partnership. 
These are the dimensions the BLI captures that purely economic indexes miss, 
and they are the ones currently most at risk.

**Inequality** is the second major concern. The persistent gaps by education, 
gender, and income mean that the benefits of the reciprocity loop are not 
being distributed equitably — which the Directive explicitly identifies as 
a failure condition.

## A Note on the BLI's Structure

Unlike the SPI, the BLI does not produce a single ranked list of countries. 
It is an interactive tool — rankings shift depending on which dimensions the 
user weights most heavily. For the purposes of this cache, the *How's Life? 
2024* companion report is the primary data source, as it provides the 
trend analysis and specific findings that the interactive tool alone does not.

## Notes & Caveats

- The BLI covers only the 38 OECD member countries — it is not a global 
  index. For non-OECD countries, the SPI provides better coverage.
- Because the BLI is user-weighted rather than fixed-ranked, it resists 
  simple comparison over time. The *How's Life?* report is the more 
  analytically rigorous companion source.
- The index was updated in 2025 to align with WISE's revised wellbeing 
  framework — earlier editions are not fully comparable.
- New indicators introduced in the 2024 edition include loneliness, 
  household energy poverty, exposure to extreme temperatures, and physical 
  pain — areas previously unmeasured.
